### Tail-sampling math

The OTel collector policy keeps all error traces and samples healthy traces. During the load run, Locust sent 1019 requests in 60 seconds, about 16.98 traces/sec. With a 1% healthy-trace policy and no request failures in the load test, the expected retained healthy traces are about `1019 * 0.01 = 10.19` traces, or roughly 10-11 traces. If there are `E` error traces, the expected retained count is `E + 0.01 * (N - E)`.

### Which test fits which feature?

For `prompt_length`, I would use PSI for dashboarding and KS for alert confirmation because it is a continuous but easily binned distribution with a clear shift in prompt population.

For `embedding_norm`, I would use KS for the scalar norm and MMD for the full embedding vector in production. Norm alone can miss directional semantic drift, while MMD can compare high-dimensional embedding distributions.

For `response_length`, PSI is good for a stable operational panel because the business meaning is bucket-friendly: short, normal, verbose, or runaway output. KS is useful as a statistical backstop.

For `response_quality`, KL or PSI can show distribution movement, but I would alert conservatively with KS plus evaluation thresholds because quality scores are bounded and often non-normal.

## 6. The single change that mattered most

The single change that mattered most was making the app emit AI-specific signals next to normal RED metrics: `inference_tokens_total`, `inference_quality_score`, and the request latency histogram. A plain service dashboard can say the API is fast and available, but it cannot say whether the model is getting more expensive, producing lower-quality responses, or drifting under a new prompt distribution. Adding those fourth-pillar metrics changes the stack from infrastructure monitoring into AI operations monitoring.

The design also avoids high-cardinality labels. The metrics keep `model`, `status`, and token `direction`, but do not label by prompt text, trace ID, or user input. That is the difference between a dashboard that works during a demo and a dashboard that remains queryable under load. This connects directly to the deck's Prometheus/cardinality guidance and the SLO section: the metric is only useful if it can be aggregated reliably enough to drive burn-rate alerts and operational decisions.
